About

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Geographic Information Science (GIScience) at the School of Geographical Sciences and Urban Planning at Arizona State University, with an expected completion in Spring 2027. My dissertation focuses on machine learning and spatial statistical modeling for investigating air temperature variation and exposure patterns, advised by Prof. David J. Sailor and co-advised by Prof. Aaron B. Flores.

My academic journey spans two continents. I earned a Bachelor of Urban and Rural Planning from Khulna University, Bangladesh; an M.Sc. in Geography from Auburn University; and an M.Sc. in Geoinformatics & Geospatial Intelligence from George Mason University.

I serve as a Graduate Research Assistant at the Urban Climate Research Center, contributing to the DOE-funded Southwest Urban Integrated Field Laboratory (SW-IFL) — a multi-million dollar initiative uniting Arizona's three public research universities, national laboratories, and community stakeholders. I am also an FAA-certified drone pilot and have reviewed proposals for NASA and panels for Columbia University.

Beyond research, I actively shape the academic community as a session chair, co-chair, and organizer at the American Association of Geographers (AAG) and the American Geophysical Union (AGU), and mentor the next generation of urban scientists.

Current Research Project
Southwest Urban Corridor Integrated Field Laboratory (SW-IFL)
The SW-IFL seeks to engage stakeholders and provide scientists and decision-makers with high-quality, timely research capable of spurring and guiding responses to environmental challenges. The integration of high-resolution observations — atmospheric, land surface, and infrastructure — with diagnostic and predictive models and civic engagement will deliver next-generation predictive tools that are regionally specific but translatable to other arid regions. Ultimately, these tools will empower the public to respond to extreme heat while informing the development of policies and solutions that are effective and generalizable.
